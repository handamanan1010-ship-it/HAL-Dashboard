2. Investment Thesis (Tear Sheet)

Why now and strategic positioning

“Why Now” – Summary

Three-bullet thesis
  • The Indian government’s indigenisation push and defence spend acceleration create a domestic growth inflection for HAL.
  • HAL has secured a massive backlog (~₹1.84 lakh crore as of FY25) providing multi-year revenue visibility.
  • Global supply-chain realignment and India’s positioning as an aerospace component hub open export growth avenues for HAL.

Positioning Line

Compact stock narrative

Positioning: “India’s state-champion aerospace manufacturer, initiating with Buy to capture the decade-long defence-capex cycle.”

The thesis rests on a rare combination of: large order book, secular defence capex, export upside, and margin expansion from indigenisation, partially offset by execution and valuation risk.

3. Investment Positives

Rank-ordered growth and profitability drivers

1) Large Order Book & Backlog Visibility

Multi-year revenue runway
  • FY 2023-24 order book: ~₹94,129 crore as of 31 March 2024 (company filing).
  • FY 2024-25 backlog expanded to ~₹1.84 lakh crore (~₹184,000 crore) driven by new orders of ~₹1.02 lakh crore (manufacturing) and ~₹17,500 crore (ROH).
  • FY25 revenue provisional ~₹30,400 crore implies book-to-bill of ~6x, providing strong revenue visibility over 3-5 years.

The order book includes large programmes such as LCH helicopters and other aircraft, ensuring a stable production pipeline.

2) Domestic Demand Tailwinds

Indian defence and aerospace capex cycle
  • India’s aerospace market expected to grow from ~US$27 billion in 2024 to ~US$54 billion by 2033.
  • “Make in India – Defence” and import substitution initiatives materially expand HAL’s total addressable market.
  • Major contract wins (e.g. ₹62,777 crore for 156 LCH helicopters) reinforce HAL’s central role in India’s defence modernisation.

Domestic orders are the primary growth engine in the near-term, with structural policy support limiting demand risk.

3) Indigenisation & Margin Expansion Potential

From licensed production to higher value add
  • HAL is transitioning from licensed manufacturing to indigenous systems (engines, avionics), increasing the value of content per aircraft.
  • FY 2023-24 PBT increased 57% to ~₹10,198 crore, while PAT grew 31% to ~₹7,595 crore, signalling improving profitability.
  • Operating leverage from scale and export expansion can lift EBITDA margins from ~10% toward ~12% over the next three years.

Structural margin upside is a key part of the re-rating story, assuming consistent execution on indigenisation.

4) Export & Global Supply-Chain Opportunity

Optionality beyond domestic orders
  • HAL has been selected to manufacture India’s Small Satellite Launch Vehicle (SSLV) with an initial contract value of ~US$59 million.
  • Geopolitical shifts and supply-chain diversification away from China favour India as an aerospace component hub.
  • HAL aims to increase export revenue via aircraft, helicopters, engines and components, targeting double-digit export mix over time.

Export growth is an upside lever, but still early stage relative to HAL’s domestic-centric revenue base.

5) Strategic National Importance & Ownership

Supportive policy environment
  • HAL is majority government-owned and designated as a “Maharatna” public sector enterprise, which offers autonomy and strategic backing.
  • Its central role in India’s defence ecosystem makes it a natural beneficiary of rising defence budgets and long-term capability building.

This strategic positioning reduces demand risk but does not eliminate execution or capital allocation risks.

Competitive Landscape

Key qualitative comparisons

Key differentiators vs global peers:

  • India’s defence manufacturing ecosystem is less mature; HAL is building supply-chain and quality systems.
  • Export access is nascent compared with Western peers that have decades of global relationships.
  • Technology complexity in fighter jets and engines demands sustained R&D and certification capabilities.

HAL’s core advantage lies in policy-driven domestic demand. Global players maintain an edge in export breadth, product maturity and after-market service networks.

7. Key Risks

Ranked by probability × impact

Risk Heat-Map

Execution, macro and valuation
1. Execution / Delivery Risk
Probability: High Impact: High

Large backlog requires timely production and supply-chain coordination (e.g. engine deliveries, avionics integration). Delays could compress margins and defer revenue recognition.

2. Technology / Quality Risk
Probability: Medium Impact: High

Transition to indigenous systems in high-complexity areas (fighter jets, engines, avionics) increases technical risk. Any failure or delay could damage reputation and financial performance.

3. Commodity / Input & FX Risk
Probability: Medium Impact: Medium

Volatility in special alloys and imported components, along with INR depreciation, can pressure margins given long-dated contracts.

4. Government Policy / Budget Risk
Probability: Low Impact: High

HAL is heavily dependent on Indian defence budgets and procurement decisions. Material budget delays or cuts could weigh on growth despite strong backlog.

5. Global Export / Competition Risk
Probability: Medium Impact: Medium

Scaling exports requires competing with experienced global OEMs and navigating defence trade restrictions. Underperformance here would limit upside optionality.

6. Valuation Risk
Probability: Medium Impact: Medium

Current valuations (c.37× P/E) already embed strong growth and margin expectations. Any negative surprise on execution could trigger multiple compression.
